My name is Edward, and I’m an undergrad in the class of 2024 and an Egleston Scholar at Columbia Engineering.

As of now, my main interests lie within machine learning and mathematics. My lifetime goal is to contribute to the ongoing pursuit of comprehending what exactly governs consciousness and sentience, which will hopefully happen some day (and also hopefully not create something along the lines of Ultron or Skynet).

Currently, most of my time is going into a research project with Professor Verma at Columbia’s Computer Science department and a software engineering job at DATAFLUCT, Inc which I’m a part of as a subcontractor.

I’m currently working on research on representation learning—specifically, looking at the properties of the skip-gram model and working on developing new methods for word embeddings. While there’s already been a significant amount of research done on the underlying properties of the skip-gram model, it suffices to say that we still don’t understood it too well and we don’t have good explanations as to why the model has the surprising qualities it has. In a nutshell, I want to see how certain changes in the skip-gram model affect downstream tasks and what sort of modifications lead to changes in the properties of the skip-gram model. Currently, we’re taking a shot at creating a new method for learning word embeddings that serves as an extension to the skip-gram model, but more on that to come in the future.

At DATAFLUCT, Inc, I’m currently working on developing a dialogue system API that provides BI (Business Intelligence) and data analytics results as the lone dev for the backend (and a bit of frontend) side. Apparently I can’t disclose much, but almost no ML is involved so far and most of the logic is purely heuristic. Nevertheless, it’s an eye-opening experience working with (way) more experienced developers who are working on different parts of the system.

Up until recently, I was also in Columbia Space Initiative (CSI), Columbia’s space-themed club. I was in the RASC-AL team that participates in an annual student competition to design deep space mission concepts. Specifically, I was in charge of designing the propulsion systems (more or less just reading past papers on the topic and coming up with new designs based on previously successful missions). While I was in Columbia’s Formula Racing Club (FSAE), I was working on tire simulation; specifically, applying curve-fitting and coefficient optimization on Pacejka’s Magic Formula.

In my free time, I like to program—mainly simulations that I find interesting to see what would happen and reinforcement learning agents to train on the games I make. I also like making robots and tinkering.
