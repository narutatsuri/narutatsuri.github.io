Narutatsu Ri

My name is Edward, and I’m an undergrad in the class of 2024 and an Egleston Scholar at Columbia Engineering.

My main interests are in machine learning theory and computational neuroscience, and most of my time is going into two research projects with Professor Nakul Verma at Columbia’s Computer Science department and a software engineering job at DATAFLUCT, Inc. where I work as a software engineer.

 

For research, I’m currently looking at the properties of the skip-gram model (a neural network language model, which is one of the two underlying models for the more well-known word2vec) and trying to debunk whether its properties are unique to skip-gram or if it’s derived from a more fundamental concept that other learning algorithms also inconspicuously harbor. A decent amount of work has already been done on identifying the underlying properties of the skip-gram model, but we still haven’t understood it too well even though it’s considered to be the “state-of-the-art” word embedding method.

In a nutshell, the word embeddings skip-gram produces induce the concept of analogies in vector arithmetic form (i.e. a famous example being “woman is to man as queen is to king,” and \(\vec{woman} - \vec{man} + \vec{king} \approx \vec{queen}\) holds, and \(\vec{king} - \vec{man}\) gives the representation of the concept of “royalty”), and the relationship has been proven to be strictly equal when the corpus skip-gram is trained on has certain properties (i.e., uniform word distribution instead of a Zeta distribution, which conventional natural language documents generally adhere to). I want to find out whether this property skip-gram has is unique by construction of skip-gram, or if other learning methods can also induce the concept of analogies in the word embeddings they learn when certain conditions hold.

My other research project is more development-focused; namely, I’m looking at ways to improve a learning algorithm that jointly learns dimensionality reduction (or, more specifically, feature extraction) and clustering during training time (also known as deep embedded clustering, or DEC) by replacing the dimensionality reduction part with something else.

 

At DATAFLUCT, Inc, I’m working on developing a dialogue system that provides BI (Business Intelligence) and data analytics results. The end goal is to have an API that can crunch corporate data (guilty as charged for using such a hand-wavy phrase) and give out meaningful replies to users who inquire about trends in their data.
